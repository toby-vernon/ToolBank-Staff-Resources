The Two Sides of Your Work

Effective program management requires balancing the need for stability with the drive for growth. This is achieved by understanding the distinct roles of Business As Usual (BAU) operations and aspirational Objectives and Key Results (OKRs).

The Anatomy of Strategic Work

Effective OKRs are structured to cover different types of work and are measured by clear, verifiable Key Results (KRs). This ensures a balanced approach to both innovation and optimization.

The C-I-T Work Allocation Model

Good OKR planning balances team capacity across three key areas. The focus should be on reducing chore-time to free up capacity for value-adding improvements and transformative innovations.

The Four Types of Key Results

A strong OKR uses a mix of KR types to track progress comprehensively. While all are useful, **Outcome** KRs are the most critical as they measure true impact.

Input

Measures effort and activities. (e.g., "Conduct 10 interviews")

Process

Measures milestone completion. (e.g., "Migrate legacy documents")

Output

Measures tangible deliverables. (e.g., "Generate 750 new leads")

Outcome

Measures business/user impact. (e.g., "Increase NPS to +50")

Forging Your Strategy: The 5-Step OKR Process

Follow this structured process to define, commit to, and review OKRs, ensuring they are distinct from BAUs and aligned with available team capacity.

1

Define Strategic Objectives

Identify 3-5 inspirational, qualitative goals.

2

Draft Key Results

Define 3-5 measurable, verifiable results for each Objective.

3

Assess Capacity (BAU Lock-in)

Quantify the time spent on BAUs to find what's left for OKRs.

4

Prioritize & Commit

De-scope or prioritize OKRs to fit within the available capacity.

5

Review & Score

At the end of the cycle, score KR progress transparently.

Capacity Assessment Example

A critical step is understanding how much time your team spends "keeping the lights on." This defines the real capacity available for strategic OKR work, preventing burnout and ensuring focus.

The Golden Rule of Execution

If a team is falling behind on their BAUs, the priority is always to stabilize them first, even if it means adjusting or de-scoping OKRs. Stability is the foundation for successful change.
